
## Proposal:
Covert letter, resumes and CVS have a lot of similar activities and might be created base on the same information. Would it be good to create one or more documents that describe past experiences and task in broad terms and then used them to tailor the desire document? I think past experience will not change, what change is the focus we give to past experience and activities, if only based on CV/resume/covert letters, they might limit the information we have for next iteration or forget some of the details, to remove the load to remember all the details and the margin error of forgetting important details a series of prompts in an interview format could help to capture previous experience and then use it to tailor the new documents.
Give me some arguments about this idea, different POV such as coach, HR executive and recruiter would be the most relevant but others are welcome. we want to refine the idea if it is evaluated to be a good one

## Analysis of the idea from multiple perspectives (v1)
First: grounding in the materials (what is supported)
The GMI materials already implicitly support part of your thinking:

Resumes, CVs, and cover letters all draw from the same underlying experiences (education, skills, achievements, projects), but emphasize them differently depending on purpose and audience.
This is explicit across:
gmi-s2-english-resume-and-cv-guide.pdf
gmi-s2-english-cover-letter-guide.pdf
gmi-m2-english-gmi-mentor-guide-session-2.pdf

The guides emphasize customization, keywords, and audience awareness, not inventing new experiences each time. That aligns with your idea that experience is stable; framing changes.
What the GMI materials do not explicitly prescribe (and where your idea goes beyond them) is:

Maintaining a single, comprehensive “experience source of truth”
Using a structured interview-style prompt system to capture experiences once and reuse them safely over time
That’s where your proposal becomes innovative.

Your proposal (restated cleanly)

Create one or more canonical experience documents that capture a person’s past experiences in rich, broad terms.
Then derive resumes, CVs, cover letters, and future iterations from that source—by changing focus, not memory.


This is essentially decoupling memory from presentation.
Now let’s evaluate it.
    
###  Perspective 1: Coach / Mentor POV ✅ (Strongly positive)
Argument for the idea
From a coaching standpoint, your proposal is excellent.
Why it works

It reduces cognitive load:
Students (especially early-career) often forget achievements, context, or impact when forced to rewrite from scratch. Your approach externalizes memory.

It encourages reflection and growth:
Interview-style prompts (“Tell me about a time when…”) surface nuance that bullet-point resumes suppress.

It supports iteration without loss:
GMI emphasizes iteration and tailoring. A canonical experience doc prevents “shrinking resumes” over time where content gets lost.

It aligns with how mentors actually think:
Coaches help people tell their story, not just format documents.
Coaching insight (opinion)

Most resume problems are not writing problems; they are recall and framing problems.

Your idea directly attacks that root cause.
Coach verdict: ✅ Very strong, especially for mentorship contexts.


### Perspective 2: HR Executive POV ⚠️ (Cautiously supportive)
Argument for the idea
HR leaders care about:

Consistency
Accuracy
Risk management
Fair evaluation
What they would like

A canonical experience record reduces inconsistencies across documents.
It lowers the risk of accidental misrepresentation (“Did I really do that, or did I phrase it wrong?”).
It helps candidates articulate experiences more clearly over time.
Concerns they might raise

Over-documentation:
If the experience document becomes too long or unfocused, candidates may struggle to distill it.

Governance risk:
HR would worry about candidates accidentally overstating impact if prompts are not well designed.

Not scalable inside hiring systems:
HR systems still ingest resumes, not experience repositories.
Refinement from HR POV
They would want:

Clear separation between raw experience and claims/impact
Prompts that emphasize facts, context, and evidence, not just storytelling
HR verdict: ✅ Good idea if structured carefully and used upstream of resumes—not as a replacement.


### Perspective 3: Recruiter POV ✅⚠️ (Mixed but ultimately favorable)
Argument for the idea
Recruiters live in two worlds:

ATS + speed
Human judgment under time pressure
What recruiters would like

Cleaner, more focused resumes and cover letters
Less fluff, more signal
Clear alignment between role requirements and experience
Your approach helps because:

Candidates with a deep experience base can tailor faster and better
It prevents generic resumes that don’t speak to the role
What recruiters don’t care about

Recruiter risk

If experience documents lead to overly verbose final outputs
Recruiter refinement
Recruiters would implicitly endorse this if and only if:

Final outputs remain concise
Each document answers: “Why you, for this role, right now?”
Recruiter verdict: ✅ Works well indirectly by improving final artifacts.

## Risks & how to refine the idea
Here’s how to make the idea robust, not just good:
1. Split the “experience source” into layers
Instead of one giant doc:
Layer 1 – Experience Inventory (canonical)

Role / activity
Context
What you did
Tools / skills
Outcomes
Evidence (metrics, artifacts, references)
Layer 2 – Impact Narratives

STAR-style stories
Challenges, growth, learning
Layer 3 – Presentation Outputs

Resume bullets
CV sections
Cover letter paragraphs
Academic statements
2. Use interview-style prompts intentionally
Your instinct is right. Example categories:

“Tell me about a time you solved a problem…”
“Describe a project you’re most proud of…”
“What did you learn that changed how you work?”
This reduces memory loss and improves accuracy.
3. Make “focus” an explicit step
Every derived document should answer:

Who is the audience?
What decision are they making?
What evidence do they need?

✅ Strongly. It supports reflection, preparation, and iteration without contradicting any official guidance.
